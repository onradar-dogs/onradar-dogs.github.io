Why Dachshunds Have Unique Bed Requirements

Dachshunds were originally bred in Germany to hunt badgers underground, which is why they have that signature long body and short, powerful legs. That same body shape — technically called chondrodystrophy — is the root cause of their elevated spinal vulnerability. Their elongated vertebral column places ongoing stress on the intervertebral discs, especially during sleep when poor posture can worsen disc compression over many hours.

According to PetMD's overview of IVDD in dogs, early lifestyle interventions — including appropriate sleeping surfaces — can help slow the progression of disc degeneration. This means the right bed is not just about comfort; it is genuinely preventive healthcare for your dachshund.

Beyond spinal concerns, dachshunds are also natural burrowers. They instinctively seek enclosed, snug spaces for sleep, which is why cave beds and hooded bolster beds tend to be favorites with the breed. The ideal bed balances orthopedic support with that cozy, den-like feel your dachshund craves.

What to Look for in the Best Dachshund Bed

Before diving into our top picks, here are the key features we evaluated during our testing process:

  • Orthopedic foam support: At minimum 2–4 inches of memory foam or high-density foam to support the spine along its full length.
  • Appropriate length: Standard dachshunds need at least 24 inches of sleeping space. Miniature dachshunds need 18–20 inches.
  • Washable cover: Dachshunds are low to the ground and collect dirt, dander, and odors quickly. Machine-washable covers are non-negotiable for most owners.
  • Non-slip bottom: Dachshunds jumping on and off beds can injure their backs. A grippy underside reduces sliding and slipping.
  • Entry height: Low or no-lip entry points reduce the temptation for your dachshund to leap awkwardly onto the bed.
  • Burrowing-friendly design: Blanket pockets, hooded canopies, or cave-style construction satisfy the breed's natural digging instinct.

Are Orthopedic Beds Good for Dachshunds With IVDD?

Absolutely — and this point cannot be overstated. Intervertebral disc disease is the number-one health concern for dachshunds, affecting up to 25% of the breed at some point in their lifetime. An orthopedic memory foam bed does two important things: it distributes the dog's body weight evenly across the full length of their spine, and it eliminates pressure points that develop when a dog sleeps on a thin or uneven surface for hours at a time.

If your dachshund has already been diagnosed with IVDD, consult your veterinarian before selecting a bed. Many vets recommend a flat, firm orthopedic surface rather than a round or nest-style bed, as a flat surface encourages the spine to rest in a neutral position. You should also place the bed on the floor rather than elevated on furniture to eliminate any jumping risk. For more information on IVDD management in daily life, the AKC's dachshund breed health guide is a helpful resource.

Are Dachshunds Hard to Fit for Beds?

Yes, more so than most breeds. The challenge is their disproportionate body — relatively long for their weight class, with a low-slung frame. Standard dog bed sizing charts are built around square or near-square body proportions, which means a bed labeled "small" may be the right weight class for a 16 lb dachshund but completely wrong in terms of length. Always measure your dachshund from nose to base of tail while they are lying fully stretched out, then add 4–6 inches to that measurement to find your minimum bed length. For most standard dachshunds (16–32 lbs), this puts you in the 24–30 inch range. Miniature dachshunds (under 11 lbs) typically need 18–22 inches.

How to Keep Your Dachshund's Bed Clean and Long-Lasting

Dachshunds are low-to-the-ground dogs, which means they track in more floor-level debris — dust, pollen, and bacteria — than taller breeds. A clean bed is not just about smell; it directly impacts your dog's skin health and respiratory comfort. Here is a simple maintenance schedule:

  • Weekly: Remove and machine-wash the cover on warm/gentle cycle. Use a pet-safe, fragrance-free detergent.
  • Monthly: Spot-clean the foam insert with a damp cloth and mild soap. Allow to air dry completely before replacing the cover.
  • Every 12–18 months: Assess the foam for compression and flattening. If your hand sinks all the way to the floor when pressing on the center, the bed has lost its orthopedic value and should be replaced.
  • Ongoing: Keep a washable waterproof liner between the foam and the outer cover to extend the life of the foam insert.

Dachshund Bed Safety: What to Avoid

Not every dog bed on the market is appropriate for a dachshund's unique needs. Here are common mistakes dachshund owners make when buying beds:

  • Beds with high entry bolsters on all four sides: These force your dachshund to jump in and out repeatedly, which is one of the leading causes of acute IVDD flare-ups.
  • Elevated or raised beds: While raised cot-style beds work well for many breeds, dachshunds should avoid them due to the jump-down risk.
  • Overly soft or plush-only beds with no foam base: These look comfortable but offer zero spinal support and can cause the spine to sag during long sleep sessions.
  • Beds that are too small: A dachshund forced to curl tightly because their bed is too short will wake up with unnecessary spinal tension.
  • Electric heated beds without auto-shutoff: These are a burn risk for dogs left unsupervised. Always choose auto-shutoff models.

Frequently Asked Questions About Dachshund Beds

What size bed does a dachshund need?

Most standard dachshunds need a bed that is at least 24 inches long, while miniature dachshunds can fit comfortably in an 18–20 inch bed. Because dachshunds have elongated bodies, prioritize length over width when comparing sizes. A bed that is too short forces them to curl awkwardly, which can strain their already-vulnerable spine. Look for beds labeled "small" to "medium" with length measurements of at least 24 inches for standard dachshunds, and always measure your dog from nose to tail before purchasing.

Are orthopedic beds good for dachshunds?

Yes, orthopedic beds are highly recommended for dachshunds. According to AKC data, dachshunds are 10–12 times more likely to develop intervertebral disc disease (IVDD) than other breeds. Orthopedic memory foam beds distribute weight evenly along their long spine, reducing pressure on intervertebral discs during the many hours your dog spends sleeping. Vets frequently recommend orthopedic sleeping surfaces as part of both IVDD prevention strategies and post-diagnosis management. They are especially important for dachshunds over age 3, overweight dachshunds, and any dog with a confirmed spinal condition.

How often should I wash my dachshund's bed?

You should wash your dachshund's bed cover at least once every 1–2 weeks under normal conditions. If your dachshund spends time outdoors, has skin allergies, or is prone to accidents, increase washing frequency to once per week. The inner foam insert can be spot-cleaned monthly and should be allowed to air dry completely before the cover is replaced. Look for beds with machine-washable, zip-off covers to make this routine as easy as possible. Waterproof liners placed between the foam and the cover can significantly extend the life of the foam insert.

Do dachshunds need a heated bed?

Dachshunds can benefit noticeably from heated beds, especially in colder climates or during winter months. Their low ground clearance and short legs mean they sit very close to cold floors, losing body heat faster than taller breeds. Senior dachshunds and those with IVDD or arthritis often find warmth particularly soothing for stiff, achy joints. Look for beds with self-warming reflective technology for everyday use, or low-wattage electric heating elements with an automatic shutoff feature for supervised use. Avoid leaving electric heating pads on unsupervised for extended periods.

Are bolster beds or flat beds better for dachshunds?

Both styles work well for dachshunds depending on your individual dog's sleeping habits and health status. Bolster (donut or cave-style) beds are excellent for dachshunds that love to burrow and feel enclosed — a deeply ingrained instinct in the breed. Flat orthopedic beds are better suited for dachshunds recovering from IVDD surgery or those who naturally sleep fully stretched out, as they encourage the spine to rest in a neutral alignment. Many experienced dachshund owners keep one of each style so their dog can choose based on mood and comfort level.

Can a dachshund sleep in a crate with a bed?

Yes, and it is actually an excellent setup for most dachshunds. Placing a supportive bed or orthopedic mat inside a crate gives your dachshund both the spinal support they need and the enclosed, den-like environment that suits their natural instincts. Choose a flat, non-slip orthopedic pad sized to fit the crate floor snugly, and avoid beds with raised bolsters if crate space is limited. Make sure the crate itself is appropriately sized — large enough for your dachshund to stand, turn, and lie fully stretched out.
